STAGE 1Weeks 1–3 · Build & foundations

Get a credible lite demo in front of people, the company on solid footing, and the first conversations started.

Lite portal / demo version W1–3

  • Lock MVP scope: G98/G99 application form, DNO-by-postcode lookup, PDF export
  • Deploy backend + Postgres (Render or Railway) from existing repo
  • Seed and verify the postcode → DNO mapping data
  • Load a sample application for a clean guided walkthrough
  • Stand up the admin dashboard to review submissions
  • Add light access-gating so the demo can be shared safely
  • End-to-end smoke test: submit → PDF → admin view

Company setup & structure W1–2

  • Confirm entity (standalone Ltd vs under the existing group)
  • Open business bank account
  • Agree founder roles & responsibilities
  • Baseline legals: T&Cs, privacy policy, GDPR data-handling for application data
  • Check insurance needs (PI/PL) given grid-connection advice

Domain secured & linked W1

  • Choose and register the domain
  • Set up DNS and a professional email inbox
  • Issue SSL and point the domain at a holding/demo page

Initial customer conversations W1–3

  • Build a target list: solar installers, EPCs, DNO-facing consultants
  • Write a short discovery script and key questions
  • Book and run 5–10 discovery calls
  • Capture pain points, current process cost, willingness to pay
STAGE 2Weeks 3–5 · Brand, pricing & site

Turn the demo and early signal into a positioned product with a brand, a price, and a site ready to launch.

Branding & messaging W3–5

  • Finalise logo, mark and the lime/energy visual system
  • One-page brand guidelines (colour, type, tone of voice)
  • Core value proposition and one-liner
  • Messaging pillars and objection handling

Confirm market entry strategy W3–4

  • Pick the beachhead segment (e.g. domestic G98 installers vs commercial G99)
  • Decide channel: direct vs partner (wholesalers, DNOs)
  • Position against incumbents using the competitor analysis
  • Define the wedge: speed/cost vs manual DNO applications

Pricing model discussions W3–5

  • Choose structure: per-application, subscription, or tiered
  • Benchmark against consultant fees and manual processing cost
  • Set launch / pilot pricing and any early-bird discount
  • Pressure-test pricing in the discovery calls

LinkedIn & social setup W4

  • Create the company LinkedIn page (logo, banner, about)
  • Align founder profiles to the new positioning
  • Draft a week-1 content calendar
  • Build reusable post/visual templates

Website build & launch prep W4–5

  • Define site structure and write copy: home, product, pricing, about, contact
  • Build on the secured domain in the brand system
  • Add demo CTA and lead-capture form
  • Wire up analytics and SEO basics; pre-launch QA
STAGE 3Weeks 5–6 · Launch & sell

Go live, make noise, and convert the early conversations into onboarded, paying customers.

Website launch W5–6

  • Final QA: mobile, performance, forms, links
  • Go live and verify analytics is tracking
  • Confirm demo and lead-capture paths work end-to-end

Social rollout W5–6

  • Publish the launch announcement
  • Founder posts plus direct outreach on LinkedIn
  • Begin steady cadence from the content calendar

Demo / customer onboarding W5–6

  • Define a simple pilot onboarding flow
  • Set up a support and feedback loop
  • Map the demo → paid conversion path

Sales & outreach activity W5–6

  • Run an outbound campaign to the target list
  • Follow up the Stage 1 discovery conversations
  • Track pipeline in a lightweight CRM
  • Aim for first signed pilot(s) by end of Week 6
★

Key milestones

  • End W1Domain live; company structure agreed; discovery calls booked.
  • End W3Lite demo shareable end-to-end; first customer conversations done; market segment chosen.
  • End W5Brand, messaging and pricing locked; LinkedIn live; website built and in pre-launch QA.
  • End W6Website launched; social rollout underway; onboarding live; first pilot(s) in motion.

Stages 1–2 and 2–3 deliberately overlap on the boundary weeks (W3, W5) so brand/pricing work can begin off the back of early demo feedback rather than waiting for a hard handover.
